How did U.S. newspaper publications across different regions foster a sense of unity during the early stages of the Cold War from the 1940 to 1960, in relation to the regional population engaged in public administration?

Main Data Code Reference

Main Content

Abstract

This project aims to explore the usage of vocabularies that foster a sense of unification throughout the early stages of the Cold War. Specifically, it aims to answer the questions of whether the escalation of Cold War and the occupational distribution of the region was was reflected by the usage of vocabularies. Case studies will be conducted on the U.S. Census Bureau - designated regions, with both qualitative and quantitative analysis on the collected data, in order to assess the intuitive hypothesis that the sense of unification fostered by the newspaper will be reflected by the increase in number of people engaging in public administrations. This project will conclude with answers to above questions, as well as a compare-and-contrast to the answers provided by a generative AI.

The Cold War, starting from the 1947 and ending in 1991, is often regarded as the most pivotal moment of shaping the modern society. In the time of such heightened tension, unification among citizens is a critical factor, which is often fostered through media outlets such as newspapers. Particularly, the linguistic choices used in these newspapers was the major device for conveying messages, varying according to the demographic details of the region of publication. In want of a closer inspection on whether the United States (U.S.) newspaper publications had adopted this method, this project aims to explore and analyze the usage of vocabularies that foster a sense of unification and their regional differences, specifically the population engaged in public administration. The project hopes to determine methods used by the U.S. newspaper for intra-national connection throughout the early stages of Cold War.

Analysis by Regions

For the analysis of each region, the optical character recognition (OCR, automated text transfer) generated U.S. newspapers published between 1940 and 1960, divided by the regions, were collected. The extracted vocabularies from the data were then filtered using Named Entity Recognition - which is extracting 'important' terms from linguistic strings - feature of SpaCy, an open-source Natural Language Processing tool. For identifying the vocabularies that were used to foster a sense of unification, thesauruses as well as manual thematic analysis on the refined data were extensively used. These were the identified vocabularies (I.D.) in random sizes:

The refined vocabularies were transformed into word graphs, where each node indicates a vocabulary and its size denotes its relative frequency (larger nodes indicate higher frequency). The edges signify that there is certain correlation between the vocabularies, which was calculated by the cosine similarity between word vectors. The word vectors, which is the representation of the words in a high-dimensional domain, was created using Google news corpus. High cosine similarity indicates a significant semantic similarity between the words. The colors of the nodes indicate the emotion conveyed in the vocabulary, which was identified using sentiment analysis - a Natural Language Processing technique - with a corpus of 124M Twitter data from 2018 to 2021. Blue indicates positive sentiment, red indicates negative sentiment, and gray indicates neutral sentiment.

Conclusion

Throughout the project, the question of whether, and how the U.S. newspaper fostered a sense of unification was explored. The occupation distribution of the regions of the U.S. from the 1940s to 1960s were visited, as well as the relevant historical newspapers transferred to textual data by the Library of Congress. The era between the 1940s and 1960s was a moment of tension for the U.S. - they were largely convinced by the illusional ‘missile gap’ between them and the Soviet Union, and were shocked by the launch of Sputnik in 1957 and Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962. (Arms Race: Definition, Cold War & Nuclear Arms | HISTORY, 2009) However, the outcome of the analysis on the textual data failed to portray such context. Words related to this heightened tension, for example “missile”, or “cuba” couldn’t be identified. The occupational distribution does show some kind of relationship to the tension, as the population of people engaging in public administration did increase. Though, the provided context is insufficient for a concrete justification. The analyzed correlation between the occupation distribution and the textual newspaper data were also rather insufficient for providing a concrete answer to the question.

Nonetheless, the important getaway from the project is that the newspapers did show some traits of emphasis on specific concepts like “family” and “war”, supporting that they did promote a social, community-wise cohesion. The differences of those specific concepts between regions visualized throughout the project indicate that the U.S. newspapers influenced and were influenced by the local issues from the 1940s to 1960s. This indirectly demonstrates their contribution to fostering a sense of unification and creating ‘shared beliefs’ in low-level contexts.

Limitations and Future Works

Limitations:

  • Small sample bias due to the relatively small dataset might have made the conclusions in this project to be highly vulnerable to the outliers or irrelevant data. This would have made the statistical analysis, such as Pearson Correlation, to be rather irrelevant.
  • The vocabularies were analyzed without context, that is, the function of each vocabularies were not identified, due to difficulties in acquiring the suitable data for such analysis, which would limit the justification of the conclusions. For example, the words used in advertisements, such as "big" or "good" might have blurred the core correlation between datasets.
  • Bias from the scope of data might have affected the conclusion. The newspapers were collected by regions, though the data provided by Library of Congress seemed to be focused on specific states, rather than the U.S. Census regions, limiting the generalization of the conclusion.
  • Errors within sentiment analysis do exist, as the corpus was composed of Tweets. The term 'children' being identified as a negative word might have resulted from tweets regarding crimes on children, which is often rapidly shared on social network services.
  • The analysis was based on the frequency of the words, meaning that 'critical' words that directly mention a particular event in the Cold War, for example the Cuban Missile Crisis, might have been blurred by the constantly and repetitively used words like "church".

Future works:

  • There seems to be a significant correlation between regions on their shared beliefs. Upon analyzing the vocabularies used in newspapers, similar words appeared. Diversifying the regions and studying the linguistic similarity between them might have been intersting.
  • Similarly, there seems to be a significant relationship between the U.S. citizens and religion, as the term "church" was the most frequently used term overall, across all regions. Conducting a study on effect of religion on U.S. citizens by using newspapers might have been interesting.
  • There seems to be some kind of relationship between the regions and their occupations. Regions close to each other showed similar traits in occupation distribution, especially the North Central and Northeast. Conducting a study on it might have been interesting.
